The Statue of Liberty

The Statue of Liberty was a ten year project beginning in 1876 - the centennial of American independence. It was a gift of international friendship from the people of France to the people of the United States as a result of the Union's victory and the signing of the 13th Amendment which abolished slavery. It was developed by Edouard de Laboulaye, a French historian and abolitionist, and crowdfunded by the French and American public. The internal construction was built by civil engineer Gustave Eiffel, who would later build the Eiffel Tower. The statue was a way to honor the United States' steps towards freedom and justice, and also to encourage France towards democracy.

Located on 12-acre Liberty Island in New York Harbor, the figure is modeled after the Roman goddess Libertas. The torch she holds high represents the enlightenment of the world. Her right foot is raised as if she is continually on the move, forever bringing freedom to others. The broken chains at her feet symbolize the United State breaking free from tyranny, and the tablet in her left hand has “July 4, 1776” inscribed on it in Roman numerals.

Before air travel, ships would sail into New York Harbor and Lady Liberty would welcome their passengers, many of which were immigrants traveling to the United States for the first time. Lady Liberty was one of the first sites they would see when sailing into the harbor, representing a symbol of freedom and hope.
